Thank You to All Who Attended!

Thank you to all who attended the seventh annual California Cognitive Science Conference (CCSC) held on Saturday May 2, 2015 at UC Berkeley's Stanley Hall. The theme for CCSC 2015 was Communication: The Connected Mind. We hope you enjoyed the excellent presentations from our speakers and poster presenters (we know we did!).

For those of you who missed it, the CCSC is an annual all-day symposium bringing together hundreds of students, researchers, and members of the general public from around the world who are passionate about the interdisciplinary field of Cognitive Science. We feature talks given by prominent scientists and thinkers from a wide variety of disciplines. Our acclaimed poster session provides undergraduates with the opportunity to present their own original research alongside graduate students and professional researchers.

Be sure to like our facebook page for the conference. Also, if you would like to receive information about future conferences join our conference mailing list. Videos of the talks can be found below (coming soon).

Keynote Speakers

Robert Knight

The Neurobiology of Language: Insights from Direct Cortical Recordings

Dr. Robert Knight is a Professor of Psychology and Neuroscience at UC Berkeley and a Professor of Neurology and Neurosurgery at UCSF. He also serves as the Director Emeritus of the Helen Wills Neuroscience Institute at UC Berkeley, the Chief Editor of the science journal Frontiers for Young Minds, and the Chief Science Advisor at NeuroFocus, Inc. Dr. Knight’s lab uses a variety of methods, including electroencephalography, fMRI, and behavioral analysis, to study how areas of the brain, in particular the prefrontal cortex, are involved in human behavior. For his outstanding research in neurology, Dr. Knight was awarded the Jacob Javits Neuroscience Investigator Award from the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke. Widely considered an expert in neurology, neurophysiological, and cognitive processing, Dr. Knight continues to make groundbreaking contributions to cognitive neuroscience.

Don Norman

Design: Applying Cognitive Science

Dr. Don Norman is a leader in the application of human-centered design. He studies the communication process between the object and the user to produce products and services that satisfy human and societal needs, both practical and emotional. He brings a unique mix of the social sciences and engineering to bear on everyday products. Dr. Norman was a cognitive science professor at UC San Diego (founding chair of the department) and Northwestern University, as well as cofounder of the Nielsen Norman Group and former Vice President of Apple Computer’s Advanced Technology Group. Dr. Norman is the director of The Design Lab at UC San Diego and author of 20 books. He is best known for his books “The Design of Everyday Things,” "”Emotional Designs" and “Living in Complexity." He was awarded the Benjamin Franklin medal in Computer and Cognitive Science, and was listed as “one of the world’s most influential designers” by BusinessWeek.

Focus Talk Speakers

Briankle Chang

Of Digits and Things

Dr. Briankle Chang is a professor of communication who specializes in cultural studies, media theory and criticism, and the philosophy of communication. He was born and raised in Taiwan and completed his postgraduate work at the University of Illinois. He has expressed ideas on the relationships between deconstruction (the idea of eliminating traditional assumptions) and communication in his book, Deconstructing Communication: Representation, Subject, and Economies of Exchange. He has been published in a variety of journals such as: International Philosophical Quarterly, Human Studies, Theory Culture and Society, and Cultural Politics. His current works focus on the extent of critical cultural studies being reflections of ideological determination and products of the academic market.

Tania Lombrozo

Three Mysteries of Explanation

Dr. Tania Lombrozo is an Associate Professor of Psychology at UC Berkeley and the director of the Concepts and Cognition Lab. She is also an affiliate of the Philosophy Department and a member of the Institute for Cognitive and Brain Science. Her main research interests include the role of explanations in our causal beliefs and probability judgments about the world, the cognitive basis of human moral reasoning, and other topics at the intersection of psychology and philosophy. Among many other honors, Dr. Lombrozo has received an NSF CAREER Award and a McDonnell Foundation Scholar Award in Understanding Human Cognition. She holds two bachelor’s degrees from Stanford University and a doctorate from Harvard University. Dr. Lombrozo also blogs about cognitive science and related themes for Psychology Today and NPR’s 13.7: Cosmos and Culture.

Kathleen Carley

Social Cognition: Intelligence Within and About Networks

Dr. Kathleen Carley is a professor in the School of Computer Science at the Institute for Software Research at Carnegie Mellon University. She is also the director of the Center for Computational Analysis of Social and Organizational Systems. Dr. Carley's research combines cognitive science, social networks, and computer science to address complex social and organizational problems. Her specific research areas include dynamic network analysis, computational social and organization theory, adaptation and evolution, text mining, the impact of telecommunication technologies and policy on communication, and information diffusion. She is the Founding Co-Editor with Al. Wallace of the journal Computational Organization Theory and has co-edited several books in the computational organizations and dynamic network area.

William Hanks

Deixis, Accessibility, and Joint Attention

Dr. William Hanks is the Distinguished Chair in Linguistic Anthropology at the University of California, Berkeley. His work is specialized in the area of Yucatec Maya language and culture, with particular emphasis on the cognitive and linguistic aspects of this field. In 2010, the American Anthropological Association honored Professor Hanks with the Edward Sapir Book Award for his historical analysis of the crucial role played by language during the Spanish colonization of the Yucatec Maya, Converting Words: Maya in the Age of the Cross. He completed his post-graduate work at the University of Chicago and has served as a Milton H. Wilson Professor of the Humanities at Northwestern University in addition to his work at the University of California, Berkeley.

Gary Dell

What Freud Got Right About Speech Errors

Dr. Gary Dell is a professor of Linguistics and Psychology at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign and a full-time faculty member at the Beckman Institute. He received his Ph.D. from the University of Toronto in 1980. His work investigates how people produce and understand sentences, and how these processes can be modelled using neural networks. For example, his research on language production concerns mechanisms of lexical information retrieval, "Freudian" influences on slips, and attempts to locate neural network parameters in the brain. He has developed a neural net model that makes predictions about the qualitative and quantitative properties of speech errors. A particularly interesting aspect of the model is that it can be used to understand patterns of behavior resulting from brain damage.

Eve Sweetser

Enacting Multiple Viewpoints Simultaneously in Language and Co-speech Gesture: How to be Yourself, Your Past Self, and Someone Else All At Once

Dr. Eve Sweetser is a professor of linguistics at the University of California, Berkeley. Her primary research interests include historical linguistics, semantics and meaning changes, the semantics of grammatical constructions, and cognitive linguistics. She has also done extensive work on metaphor and iconicity, subjectivity and viewpoint, the relationship between language and gesture, and the Celtic language family. She received her Ph.D. in Linguistics from UC Berkeley in 1984, and has been a member of the Berkeley faculty since that time. She has served as Director of Berkeley's undergraduate Cognitive Science Program and is currently Director of the Celtic Studies Program. She has published articles on topics including modality, polysemy, metaphor, conditional constructions, grammatical meaning, performativity, gesture, and Medieval Welsh poetics.
